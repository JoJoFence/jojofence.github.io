Whole-Body Humanoid Control via Reinforcement Learning

Background

Reinforcement learning (RL) has emerged as a powerful paradigm for training complex robotic behaviors that are difficult to specify through traditional control methods. This project focuses on training a simulated Unitree G1 humanoid robot to perform whole-body locomotion and upper-body manipulation tasks using deep RL in NVIDIA Isaac Lab. The goal is to enable the robot to walk to target locations while simultaneously performing precise reaching and grasping motions with its arms — a key capability for real-world humanoid deployment in environments designed for humans.

Unlike locomotion-only policies or fixed-base manipulation, whole-body control requires coordinating the legs for balance and movement with the arms for task execution, all under a single unified policy. This presents unique challenges in reward shaping, action space design, and sim-to-real transfer.

Implementation

The system is built on NVIDIA Isaac Lab with the Unitree G1 humanoid model (29 DoF). The RL policy is trained using PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) via the RSL-RL framework, running thousands of parallel environments on GPU for fast training.

The training pipeline follows this high-level architecture:

Observations → Policy → Actions: The actor-critic MLP takes as input the robot's joint positions, joint velocities, projected gravity vector, and target commands (desired base velocity and end-effector reach targets). The actor network outputs joint position targets tracked by a low-level PD controller, while the critic estimates value for PPO updates.

Reward Shaping & Metrics: The reward function balances multiple competing objectives. Key training metrics tracked during optimization include:

• Mean reward per episode — overall training signal convergence

• End-effector reach error — distance (m) between hand and target position

• Base velocity tracking error — deviation from commanded walking speed

• Episode length — longer episodes indicate stable policies (no falling)

• Success rate — percentage of episodes where both reach and locomotion targets are met within tolerance

Curriculum learning progressively increases task difficulty (larger reach targets, faster velocity commands) as the policy improves, preventing the agent from getting stuck in local optima.

Current Status: The project is currently in the upper-body hardware testing phase. As shown in the GIF above, the trained reaching and grasping policy is being validated on the real Unitree G1 torso, testing end-effector tracking accuracy and wrist control under real-world dynamics. Once the upper-body manipulation is robust on hardware, the next phase will integrate whole-body locomotion RL — training the legs to walk while the arms simultaneously execute reaching and grasping tasks.

RoboMime: A Human Pose Imitation System for Robotic Teleoperation

A Bit of Background

Human-Robot Interaction, or HRI, concerns the design, testing, and evaluation of the relationship between robotic systems and humans. It examines the question of how to best engineer robots such that they might be more widely accepted in human society. The HRI Laboratory at Kyoto University is one of a handful of places at the forefront of addressing this very question. Specifically, its goal is to run experiments to determine how to best integrate robots into public spaces, such as shopping malls, stores, and airports. My research at the HRI Lab during the summer of 2023 involved implementing a novel method of making the human-robot interaction experience feel more “familiar” to the human. I was tasked with creating a research question and project on my own, so the majority of my work was self-initiated and original. Ultimately, I decided to focus on addressing a current problem I saw with how the robots in the lab were being operated.

Description

In the figure above, I am testing out my program using a robot called Robovie. Robovie consists of a wheeled drive at its base, two arms with rotating joints at the shoulders and elbows, and a head that can rotate around its neck axis, tilt side to side, and tilt back and forth. This robot has been used for HRI experiments for nearly twenty years, but what surprised me was that the majority of these experiments barely took advantage of its very capable arms and head. Many would just have the upper body completely static, while others might simply have a few buttons on a console assigned to specific movements (such as a “point” button where Robovie would raise its right arm, or a “neck pivot” button where Robovie would turn its head). This highlighted another issue: that the teleoperator interface for the robot was not very intuitive. Why have a joystick and complicated buttons for a robot with a near-humanoid form? Is the point of HRI not to make robots as human-friendly and intuitive as possible? I therefore sought to address these concerns by designing a new teleoperator interface, one where the user could simply move their arms and head as they would naturally when interacting with another human and the robot would mimic these movements as closely as its mechanical limits would allow. Naturally, I decided to call this program “RoboMime.”

Implementation

When debating how I wanted to go about this project, I wanted to ensure that it would be accessible and easy to implement for anyone who might need to use it. This meant no fancy suits or hardware for tracking the movements of the human operator. I figured the best way to achieve this would be to use a single camera so that even the average hobbyist could use this software at their desk at home without the need for an array of cameras in a separate motion-tracking environment. This is tricky to do, because in order to accurately track a human, you need to know their body joint coordinates and orientations in three dimensions, something traditionally achieved by having multiple cameras to triangulate points in 3D-space. However there are cameras that contain depth sensing capabilities, the most famous one probably being the Xbox Kinect camera used for gaming. And it just so happened that the HRI Lab at Kyoto had a whole bunch of them. I decided to use the newer version of this camera called the Azure Kinect, which is fairly small, lightweight, and contains a bunch of sensors capable of depth measurements, RGB scanning, and infrared emission.

I set up this camera to track the skeletal joints of humans it found in its view frame and wrote a series of Python scripts to calculate joint angles in real-time and convert them to the reference frame and data format used for the Robovie robot. I then developed a simulator (as shown in the image above) that would receive the converted data and show how Robovie would move based on the human operator’s movements. After a great deal of testing and debugging using the simulator, I moved on to using the real robot (as shown in the image above the Description section). The code and instructions for how to replicate this project can be found at the GitHub link below!

Gazed-Based Deep Imitation Learning for Robot Manipulation

Overview and Setup

This was a research project I conducted at the Intelligent Systems and Informatics Laboratory at the University of Tokyo during the summer of 2022. I worked primarily with the robot shown in the image above which was a dual-arm teleoperated robot comprising two UR5 arms with simple grasping grippers as end effectors and two 3D stereo cameras on a dual-axis gimbal system for vision. Next to this robot sat a very similar-looking structure (two arms, grippers, camera system), but this was a custom-built “skeleton” meant to be operated by a human (see the image below). To operate the robot, a person would sit in a chair in the skeleton, put on a VR headset, and grab the skeleton’s two arms. When the person moved their head, the camera system on the robot would move accordingly, so the person would see what the robot was seeing through the VR headset. Similarly, whatever motion the person would do with skeleton’s arms, the arms of robot would copy synchronously.

Deep Imitation Learning Phase

By using the described teleoperation setup, a person could perform certain tasks over and over again, and then use this data to train the robot to perform those tasks by itself (without any teleoperation). This is where the deep imitation learning phase came in, and the novelty of this system lay in the fact that the VR headset was able to track the gaze of the person, so the robot was able to learn to “see” what objects to focus on based on the person’s gaze point. This of course added an additional layer of complexity, however made the system extremely versatile for learning to perform all sorts of tasks. Particularly, it meant that the robot was not restricted by what objects it could recognize as it was not using a pre-trained vision model. Nor was it constrained by pre-determined arm and end effector motions. Instead, it could in theory be used to find and manipulate any object as it relies solely on the gaze and teleoperation of the human training it, as long as the object is of course physically able to be manipulated given the design limitations of the arms and end effectors.

BoogieBots: Collaborative Robotics for CS Education

Description

BoogieBots are small, two-wheeled robots that I created intended to teach students about programming and robotics in a collaborative fashion. Over the course of a semester, I built and programmed two of these robots, each consisting of 3D-printed materials for much of its base parts and supports, a circuit board with components such as motor drivers and a Teensy microcontroller for controlling its motors, a Raspberry Pi 4 computer for high-level processing and wireless communication, and a Raspberry Pi camera to capture live video. Also attached to each BoogieBot is a robot face with a QR code on it. I designed a graphical user interface (GUI) accessible as a website that allows for the control of each robot. The GUI contains a live stream video view from the robot’s camera, arrow keys to control its movement, a “Take Photo” button to capture a photo from the camera, and an embedded block-based coding environment (using Blockly) for creating dance routines to send to other robots. The idea is that each student would design their own robot face, generating a QR code that embeds their name and their robot’s IP address. I decided in the end that I wanted this project to be accessible for younger students (elementary school - middle school), so instead of having the students’ build the robots, they would design their robot’s “ID card” and learn via the block-based coding environment in the GUI. When one student’s robot drives up to another and detects its QR code via the camera feed, the option comes up for the student to send a dance routine to the other robot. The student can create a routine via the Blockly interface (with blocks for moving forwards for x number of seconds, moving backwards, etc.) and send it over to the other student’s robot. Upon retrieval of the routine, the other student can decline or accept it and upon accepting it, can execute it and hit the Boogie Button. Then their robot will dance using the routine sent by the other student!

Robot Design Optimization With Shape Grammars and Deep RL

This was a research project for a computational design course I took at Harvard. Here is the problem I was trying to address and my proposed solution:

Problem

  • Finding the best design for a robot given environmental parameters is a tedious process.

  • A brute-force approach is often implemented, where physical assemblies are built, tested, and rebuilt which is costly and time consuming.

Solution

  • This project explore how shape grammars can be used to computationally optimize robotics designs prior to the physical building and testing phases.

  • A deep reinforcement learning approach is used to generate the best designs for robots tasked with traversing a simulated terrain.

  • The generative process uses shape grammar rules to construct robots comprising fundamental shapes, such as rectangles, triangles, and circles. These designs are implemented using Python and tested using a graphical simulator.

The above diagram gives a high-level overview of the development process for this project. First shape grammars are defined and shapes are parametrized such that their size and form are variable. Then a robot design is tested in the simulated environment to see how well it is able to traverse an obstacle-filled terrain. Depending on the reward gained (i.e. how far the robot travels), the design is adjusted by altering the shape parameters and/or the applied shape grammar rules. Then this new design is tested and evaluated, and this process is repeated for several thousand epochs to train a deep learning model. This model is optimized to return the ideal robot design at the end of training. Two of these designs along with their training plots are shown below!

RoboDraw: An Autonomous Drawing Robot

Overview

This project involved the design, building, and programming of a robot that could autonomously draw images on a whiteboard. The robot’s base is composed of 3D-printed materials, its “brain” is an Arduino UNO microcontroller, and its movement is controlled using two stepper motors. I also developed an iOS app using Swift where a user could select the image they wanted the robot to draw and send it to the robot via bluetooth.

Challenges and Demonstration

This project was very challenging. Converted lines from images to vector paths for the robot to follow proved a mathematical nightmare! Using SVG images helped with this task, although a simulator would have been extremely useful for testing.
